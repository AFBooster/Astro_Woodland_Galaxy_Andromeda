# Weekly Night-Sky Briefing

**Session:** Friday 10 July 2026, 7:30–10:00 pm (1930–2200 hrs)
**Location:** Science Centre Observatory (SCOB), Jurong, Singapore (1.3342°N, 103.7357°E)

## Tonight at a glance

Tonight is a genuinely dark-sky night, and that is the headline. The Moon is a thin waning crescent, only about 20% illuminated, and — better still — it does not rise until the small hours before dawn. That means there is no Moon in the sky at any point during our session, from setup right through to pack-up. New Moon falls in a few days on the 14th, so we are catching the sky at its darkest and most generous. Every faint showpiece that a bright Moon would normally wash out — nebulae, galaxies, the delicate outer stars of the globular clusters — is fully on the menu tonight. This is the week to point the big scope at deep sky and let its aperture do the work.

Planets are a quiet, brief showing. Brilliant Venus is the one evening planet, hanging low in the west after sunset; catch it early in the first hour before it slides below the horizon. Jupiter has all but disappeared into the sunset glow as it heads for solar conjunction on the 29th, and is effectively unobservable. Mars and Saturn are strictly pre-dawn objects this month and never rise during our window. With the planets thin, the real riches lie overhead in the southern Milky Way, which from Singapore's near-equatorial latitude rides as high and clear as it does anywhere on Earth — and with no Moon to fight, it will be spectacular.

## The telescopes

The observatory fields its full fleet tonight. In the main dome, the 40 cm (16") Cassegrain reflector (focal length 520 cm) is the showpiece, with its piggybacked 15 cm (6") apochromatic refractor riding alongside for wide-field, high-contrast views. The second dome runs its 11" catadioptric coupled with a 7" as a second viewing station. Out on the deck, the portable Celestron CPC-series SCTs (8"–11", computerised GoTo — no GPS, so date, time and location are keyed into the hand control manually) and the lightweight Celestron C6 (6" SCT) spread the queue across many instruments.

Because the sky is dark tonight, the 40 cm main scope finally gets to do what it does best: gather faint light. Point it at the globular cluster cores and the brighter nebulae, where its aperture resolves the clusters into a blaze of individual stars and pulls real structure out of the gas clouds. The 6" apo refractor is the instrument for wide, contrasty star fields and double stars, and shines on the sprawling open clusters of the Scorpius tail. The second dome's 11" makes an excellent second deep-sky station. The fast-slewing portable CPC and C6 SCTs are the workhorses for keeping public queues moving — send them to the bright, easy-to-find showpieces such as Omega Centauri, the Jewel Box and M6/M7, where a quick GoTo and an instant "wow" keep the line flowing.

## What to point at

Open the evening while Venus is still up: swing a GoTo scope low to the west to bag the brilliant planet before it sets in the first hour. Then turn south and stay there, because the southern sky is the star of tonight's show. Omega Centauri (NGC 5139), the finest globular cluster in the entire sky, rides high in the south and in the 40 cm resolves into thousands of pinpoint stars — with no Moon tonight it is breathtaking. Right beside it sits Centaurus A (NGC 5128), a genuine other galaxy cut by a dark dust lane, and a realistic target in the big scope on a dark night like this. Nearby, Alpha Centauri (Rigil Kentaurus) splits into a lovely matched golden pair, and low in Crux the Jewel Box (NGC 4755) and the double star Acrux reward an early look before the Southern Cross sinks toward the horizon.

Then work the Milky Way's core in the south-east, which rides high and, crucially, dark tonight. Ruddy Antares anchors Scorpius, with the bright open clusters M6 (Butterfly) and M7 (Ptolemy's Cluster) in the tail and the globular M4 right beside Antares — all superb tonight. Sweeping into Sagittarius, the Lagoon Nebula (M8) and the Trifid (M20) show real nebulosity in the 40 cm, the globular M22 is a magnificent ball of stars, and higher up the Swan/Omega Nebula (M17) and the Eagle Nebula (M16) round out a genuinely deep-sky evening. With the sky Moon-free from first light to last, there is no need to rush the faint targets — they will hold up all session.

## Weather outlook

Singapore remains under the Southwest Monsoon (roughly June to September), climatologically the drier half of the year. MSS guidance for the first fortnight of July calls for below-average rainfall over most of the island, with the thundery showers that do occur tending to fall in the late morning and afternoon, and drier conditions settling in through the second week. That is an encouraging backdrop for an evening session. Expect a warm, humid night: temperatures easing from about 31°C at 6 pm to around 28°C by 10 pm, humidity climbing from the mid-70s into the mid-80s as the evening cools, and moderate cloud cover of roughly 55–65% typical of monsoon evenings. Any showers tonight are more likely late than during our window, so the observing period has a fair chance of usable clear breaks. ## Sky map
