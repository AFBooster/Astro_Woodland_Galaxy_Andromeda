# Weekly Night-Sky Briefing

**Session:** Friday 3 July 2026, 7:30–10:00 pm (1930–2200 hrs)
**Location:** Science Centre Observatory (SCOB), Jurong, Singapore (1.3342°N, 103.7357°E)

## Tonight at a glance

This is a dark-sky night, and that is the headline. The Moon is a waning gibbous around 85% illuminated, but it does not clear the eastern horizon until roughly 9:56 pm — so for the great majority of the session, from setup right through to about 10 pm, there is no moonlight at all in the sky. That is a complete reversal of last week, when a near-full Moon flooded the sky and quietly erased the fainter showpieces. Tonight the nebulae, galaxies and globular clusters that need a genuinely dark sky are back on the menu, and this is the week to point the big scope at them. The Moon only creeps up in the east right at the very end of the session, so the last half-hour is the time to swing over and enjoy it as a fine target in its own right.

Planets are a quiet showing. Brilliant Venus sits low in the west after sunset and is a catch-early object before it sinks; Jupiter is lost in the solar glare (heading for conjunction late in the month) and is not observable. Mars and Saturn are strictly pre-dawn objects this month and never rise during our window. With the planets thin, the real riches tonight lie in the southern Milky Way — the galactic centre in Sagittarius and Scorpius rides high in the south-east, and Centaurus and Crux are beautifully placed in the south. From Singapore's near-equatorial latitude this whole southern sweep is as good as it gets anywhere on Earth, and with no Moon to fight, it will be spectacular.

## The telescopes

The observatory fields its full fleet tonight. In the main dome, the 40 cm (16") Cassegrain reflector (focal length 520 cm) is the showpiece, with its piggybacked 15 cm (6") apochromatic refractor riding alongside for wide-field, high-contrast views. The second dome runs its 11" catadioptric coupled with a 7" as a second viewing station. Out on the deck, the portable Celestron CPC-series SCTs (8"–11", computerised GoTo) and the lightweight Celestron C6 (6" SCT) spread the queue across many instruments.

Because the sky is dark tonight, the 40 cm main scope finally gets to do what it does best: gather faint light. Point it at the globular clusters and the brighter nebulae, where its aperture resolves cores into a blaze of individual stars and pulls real structure out of the gas clouds. The 6" apo refractor is the instrument for wide, contrasty star fields and double stars, and shines on the sprawling open clusters of the Scorpius tail. The second dome's 11" makes an excellent second deep-sky station. The fast-slewing portable CPC and C6 SCTs are the workhorses for keeping public queues moving — send them to the bright, easy-to-find showpieces (Omega Centauri, the Jewel Box, M6/M7), and save them for the Moon once it rises near the end of the night.

## What to point at

Open the evening while Venus is still up: swing a GoTo scope low to the west to bag the brilliant planet before it sets in the first hour. Then turn south and stay there, because the southern sky is the star of tonight's show. Omega Centauri (NGC 5139), the finest globular cluster in the entire sky, rides high in the south and in the 40 cm resolves into thousands of pinpoint stars — with no Moon tonight it is breathtaking. Right beside it sits Centaurus A (NGC 5128), a genuine other galaxy cut by a dark dust lane; drowned out last week, tonight it is a realistic target in the big scope. Nearby, Alpha Centauri splits into a lovely matched golden pair, and low in Crux the Jewel Box (NGC 4755) and the double star Acrux reward an early look before the cross sinks.

Then work the Milky Way's core in the south-east, which is loaded with showpieces and, crucially, dark tonight. Ruddy Antares anchors Scorpius, with the bright open clusters M6 (Butterfly) and M7 (Ptolemy's Cluster) in the tail and the globular M4 right beside Antares — all fine tonight. Sweeping into Sagittarius, the Lagoon Nebula (M8) and the Trifid (M20) show real nebulosity in the 40 cm, the globular M22 is a superb ball of stars, and higher up the Swan/Omega Nebula (M17) and the Eagle Nebula (M16) round out a genuinely deep-sky evening. As the session winds down after about 9:56 pm, the waning gibbous Moon rises in the east — a good moment to swing the portable scopes over to it and finish on the cratered lunar surface.

## Weather outlook

Singapore is under the Southwest Monsoon (roughly June to September), which is climatologically the drier half of the year. MSS's guidance for the first fortnight of July calls for mostly short-duration thundery showers, with below-average rainfall and above-average temperatures — an encouraging backdrop for an evening session. Expect a warm, humid night: temperatures easing from about 31°C at 6 pm to around 28°C by 10 pm, humidity in the mid-70s to mid-80s, and moderate cloud cover of roughly 50–60% typical of monsoon evenings. Any showers this fortnight tend to be brief rather than washout affairs, so the observing window has a fair chance of usable clear breaks. ## Sky map

The all-sky chart below is drawn for about 2030 hrs, looking straight up: zenith at the centre, horizon at the edge, north at the top and east to the left. The southern Milky Way dominates the lower half — Scorpius and Sagittarius blaze in the south-east with their nebulae and clusters, while Centaurus and Crux ride the south. Named bright stars include Spica (Virgo), Arcturus (Boötes), Antares (Scorpius), Vega (Lyra), Altair (Aquila), Rigil Kentaurus and Hadar (Centaurus), and Acrux (Crux). The Moon is deliberately absent — it is still below the horizon at this hour and does not rise until about 9:56 pm, which is exactly why tonight's faint targets shine.
